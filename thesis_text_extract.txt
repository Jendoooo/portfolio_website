This project presents a comprehensive computational workflow to evaluate the performance of the metal-organic framework ZIF-8 for this separation, with a primary focus on addressing the challenge of data reproducibility in the literature.A large dataset of pure-component CO₂ and CH₄ adsorption isotherms on ZIF-8 was compiled from the NIST/ARPA-E database as well as manual search from journals and subjected to a rigorous statistical screening framework. This process identified and removed outlier datasets, resulting in robust, high-reproducibility (R1 grade) consensus isotherms for both gases. The Tóth isotherm model was selected for its superior numerical fit and physical relevance to describe the consensus data.Using these validated pure-component models, Ideal Adsorbed Solution Theory (IAST) was applied to predict the binary adsorption behaviour. The IAST model predicts a strong thermodynamic preference for CO₂ over CH₄, with selectivity increasing with pressure due to competitive exclusion effects. However, when benchmarked against the sole available experimental binary data, the IAST predictions systematically underestimated the total gas loadings and failed to capture the non-monotonic selectivity trend. This discrepancy is attributed to the inherent limitation of IAST, which assumes a rigid adsorbent and cannot account for the well-documented framework flexibility ("swing effect") of ZIF-8.This study demonstrates that standard predictive models like IAST, even when based on high-reproducibility data, can underpredict significantly for flexible adsorbents, reducing total uptake by up to 30% and failing to capture non-monotonic selectivity trends observed experimentally. This highlights the critical need to incorporate framework dynamics for accurate engineering design.Keywords: Metal-Organic Frameworks (MOFs), Ideal Adsorbed Solution Theory (IAST), Adsorption Isotherms, Reproducibility Analysis, CO2/CH4 SeparationACKNOWLEDGMENTI would like to express my immense gratitude to my project supervisors, Dr Miguel Jorge, and Professor Ashleigh Fletcher. Developing efficient methods to capture CO2 from various industrial point sources is therefore a cornerstone of global sustainability efforts2. Among the available options, process based on selective adsorption onto porous solids are particularly promising. Amine-based solvent capture, while effective, incurs high regeneration energy costs, whereas solid sorbents can cut energy use by 30% or more in CO₂ desorption and offer competitive costs ($48.1–75.2/t-CO₂ compared to $62.8/t-CO₂ for MEA), making lowering regeneration temperature a key research direction3.Within this broad field, this work specifically addresses the separation of carbon dioxide (CO₂) from methane (CH₄), a process of significant industrial and environmental importance. This separation is critical in two main applications: natural gas "sweetening" and biogas upgrading4. In natural gas processing, raw gas streams often contain CO₂ which has no heating value and must be removed to increase the energy density of the final product. Furthermore, in the presence of water, CO₂ forms corrosive carbonic acid, which can damage pipelines and storage infrastructure, making its removal essential for operational safety and integrity. Similarly, biogas, a renewable energy source from anaerobic digestion, contains high concentrations of CO₂ that must be removed to produce high-grade, pipeline-compatible biomethane5. Therefore, developing efficient CO₂/CH₄ separation materials is pivotal for both enhancing fossil fuel value chains and enabling renewable energy systems.6 To achieve these separations, technologies based on adsorption onto porous solid materials are being intensively investigated due to their potential for high efficiency and lower energy consumption. Literature ReviewA Survey of Adsorbent MaterialsThe search for ideal solid adsorbents has explored several classes of materials, each with distinct advantages and drawbacks. Zeolites, crystalline aluminosilicates, are widely used due to their low cost, high thermal stability, and strong affinity for CO₂ over non-polar gases like CH₄, which arises from electrostatic interactions within their rigid pore structures7. However, their performance is severely hampered `by the presence of water, which adsorbs competitively and often necessitates an energy-intensive pre-drying step7.Activated carbons (ACs) are another low-cost option, valued for their high surface area and hydrophobicity, making them tolerant to moisture8. Their primary limitation is low CO₂ selectivity, as their non-polar surfaces interact weakly and non-specifically with gas molecules9. While their surfaces can be chemically modified to improve performance, this adds complexity and cost10. A third category, amine-functionalized silicas, combines the high selectivity of liquid amines with a solid support, offering high CO₂ capacity11. However, these materials can suffer from higher regeneration energy requirements compared to pure physisorbents and potential degradation of the amine groups over time12. This landscape of trade-offs motivates the investigation of more advanced, tunable materials.Metal-Organic Frameworks (MOFs) as Advanced AdsorbentsOne class of materials attracting special interest for CO₂ adsorption is metal–organic frameworks (MOFs). MOFs are crystalline, highly porous materials constructed from metal nodes and organic linkers, and they offer exceptional surface areas, tunable pore sizes, and chemically adjustable internal surfaces13.These properties allow MOFs to be tailored for selective gas uptake – for example, by incorporating coordinatively unsaturated metal sites or amine-functionalized linkers that bind CO₂ preferentially over non-polar gases like methane (CH₄)13. Numerous studies have explored MOFs for separating CO₂ from CH₄ (e.g. in natural gas upgrading or landfill gas recovery) and from N₂ (in flue gas from combustion) to enable cleaner fuel streams and emissions reduction. For instance, Bae and Snurr (2011) evaluated over 40 different MOFs against key performance criteria for CO₂/CH₄ and CO₂/N₂ separations, finding many candidates with high CO₂ capacities and selectivities under relevant conditions14. Such findings underscore the potential of MOFs to enable efficient post-combustion CO₂ capture and related gas separation applications.This study focuses specifically on Zeolitic Imidazolate Framework-8 (ZIF-8), a prototypical MOF that is exceptionally well-suited for investigating CO₂/CH₄ separation. ZIF-8 is composed of zinc nodes and 2-methylimidazolate linkers, forming a robust structure with a sodalite (SOD) topology15. Its relevance stems from a unique structural feature: it possesses large internal cages (11.6 Å) accessible through narrow flexible pore apertures of approximately 3.4 Å16./Figure 1 : Atomistic representation of the ZIF-8 structure17This aperture dimension is critically positioned between the kinetic diameters of CO₂ (3.3 Å) and CH₄ (3.8 Å), theoretically enabling it to act as a molecular sieve, selectively allowing the smaller CO₂ molecules to pass while hindering the larger CH₄ molecules16. This unique gate-size exclusion effect makes ZIF-8 an appealing candidate for kinetic separations, and indeed it has shown promising CO₂-over-CH₄ selectivity in several studies16. However, the pronounced diffusion differences and framework flexibility of ZIF-8 also pose challenges for accurately predicting its separation performance.The Challenge of Data ReproducibilityA major challenge in translating MOF adsorption research into practice is the variability and limited reproducibility of gas adsorption data reported by different researchers. CO₂ uptake in each MOF can be highly sensitive to sample preparation (synthesis method, activation procedure) and measurement protocol, so published isotherms from different labs often do not agree quantitatively. Park et al. (2017) highlighted this issue in a landmark meta-analysis of CO₂ isotherms on MOFs. By mining a large collection of published isotherms in the NIST/ARPA-E adsorption database, they found that very few MOFs had truly reproducible CO₂ isotherm data across multiple independent studies. In fact, out of thousands of MOFs reported in literature, only 15 materials had enough repeat measurements to consider their CO₂ isotherms consistent between research groups. Overall, roughly 20% of the CO₂ uptake data points in the database were flagged as statistical outliers. These points deviated significantly from the consensus trend and likely stemmed from experimental error or sample inconsistencies. The group concluded that much of the published adsorption data has not been independently reproduced, and they urged the community to adopt more standardized measurement protocols and reporting practices to improve reliability18.More recently, McCready et al. extended these concerns to simulation studies, demonstrating that even the selection of a force field can significantly impact predicted adsorption uptake. Their work revealed that the uncertainty in simulated methane isotherms for the same MOF can be as large as 15%, underscoring systematic shortcomings in these models. They further highlighted that inconsistent or unreliable experimental isotherm data makes it challenging to validate computational models or predict separations, emphasizing that 'the reproducibility and reliability of reported experimental isotherms can be seriously lacking, thus leading to uncertainty' in comparative analyses19. Such fundamental variability impedes the fair comparison of materials and complicates the crucial task of utilizing these data for accurate modelling and process design. Consequently, the absence of consistent single-gas isotherm data represents a significant gap when attempting to predict mixed-gas adsorption using theoretical frameworks. Predicting Mixture Adsorption and the Role of IASTOne widely used tool for predicting multicomponent adsorption equilibria from single-component data is the Ideal Adsorbed Solution Theory (IAST). Originally developed by Myers and Prausnitz in 1965, IAST provides a thermodynamic framework to estimate how a mixture of gases will be adsorbed based solely on the pure-gas isotherms of each component20. In principle, given a MOF’s CO₂ and CH₄ isotherms, one can apply IAST to predict the selectivity and uptake of CO₂ from a CO₂/CH₄ binary mixture at equilibrium21. However, if the input isotherm data are unreliable or inconsistent, the IAST predictions will likewise be highly uncertain or even misleading. A small error in the pure CO₂ isotherm, for instance, could lead to large deviations in the predicted CO₂/CH₄ selectivity, especially under conditions where one component is strongly favoured. This propagation of uncertainty is a known challenge, and methods exist to quantify its impact22. Probabilistic techniques, such as Monte Carlo simulations, are used to propagate the uncertainty from the pure-component isotherm parameters through the IAST calculations. By repeatedly sampling from the probability distributions of the input parameters, these methods can generate a full probability distribution for the predicted mixture properties, providing not just a single value but a confidence interval for the prediction23.Therefore, establishing a robust, harmonized set of CO₂ and CH₄ isotherm data is a critical prerequisite for trustworthy mixture predictions. Only with reliable single-component adsorption parameters can one confidently design an adsorption-based CO₂/CH₄ separation process around a given adsorbent. To address these reproducibility issues and provide robust CO₂/CH₄ separation predictions, this study compiles experimental isotherms, primarily focusing on adsorption data from the NIST Adsorption Database and supplemented with manual search and applies the Tóth model for single-component fitting, followed by IAST for mixture prediction. This study focuses specifically on ZIF-8, a prototypical MOF for gas separation, to address these challenges with methane and CO2 adsorption. The full methodology is outlined in the next sectionMethodology Data Collection from NIST/ARPA ServerThe foundation of this study involved compiling a comprehensive dataset of experimental adsorption isotherms for ZIF-8 with carbon dioxide (CO₂) and methane (CH₄). Data was systematically acquired from the NIST/ARPA-E Adsorption Database (NIST Standard Reference Database 205), a critical public repository for adsorption research24.The initial phase focused on efficiently identifying all relevant ZIF materials and associated isotherm records. A custom Python script was developed to interact with the NIST Materials Database (MatDB) API. This script performed a broad query for all entries containing the "ZIF" prefix, collecting basic metadata such as material names, unique Hashkeys, and reported synonyms. This initial automated scrape yielded 171 unique ZIF materials.Subsequently, the script accessed the NIST ISODB GitHub repository to extract detailed JSON records for all isotherms linked to these ZIF materials. This comprehensive extraction process gathered metadata including adsorbate type, temperature, pressure units, adsorption units, and publication details25. The broad collection resulted in a total of 256 isotherms specifically for ZIF-8 (encompassing various gases and experimental/simulated data categories) at 298-303K, a range selected to represent near-ambient temperature conditions relevant to many gas separation processes for Carbon dioxide and Methane. At this stage, the primary objective was to cast a wide net, ensuring no potentially relevant data was missed, before applying more stringent quality control and manual verification.Manual Validation and HarmonisationFollowing the initial automated data acquisition, all collected isotherm records underwent a rigorous manual validation and harmonization process. This critical step involved meticulous cross-referencing with the corresponding original scientific publications to ensure data integrity and consistency, moving beyond automated metadata tags.The validation procedure focused on several key aspects:Unit Conversion: All reported uptake values were systematically converted to mmol/g using the respective gas molecular weights, and pressure values were standardized to bar. This ensured a uniform basis for quantitative comparison across diverse experimental reports. For example, Data initially reported in mg/g was precisely converted to mmol/g by dividing by the gas's molar mass. Similarly, cm³(STP)/g was converted using the ideal gas molar volume.Data Exclusion based on Experimental Protocol: Isotherms were carefully reviewed for methodological inconsistencies that could compromise their reliability for this study. This included:Exclusion of isotherms based on relative pressure, as these often represent different adsorption regimes not directly comparable to absolute pressure isotherms.Exclusion of data explicitly derived from pure simulation, or those with ambiguous origins (e.g., "molecules per cage" data that was "calibrated by GCMC" without clear raw experimental backing), where the experimental component could not be verified as independent. This decision was made after careful cross-referencing the original publications to understand the full context of the data generation.For the ZIF-8 methane dataset, the isotherm '10.1016j.seppur.2019.115850.Isotherm8’ initially appeared valid but, upon detailed review of its source, exhibited an unphysical flat region in its low-pressure adsorption, strongly suggesting a digitization error26. This inherent selectivity is a direct consequence of fundamental physicochemical differences between the two gaseous components. The practical impact of this preference becomes starkly evident when analysing the effects of competitive adsorption. A direct comparison between the IAST-predicted loadings for an equimolar binary mixture (yCO₂ = 0.5) and the benchmark pure-component consensus isotherms is crucial./Figure 3 : Line plot comparing IAST and consensus single-component loadings for CO₂ and CH₄ at yCO₂ = 0.50As shown in Figure 3, CO₂ uptake increases steeply with pressure, achieving a loading of approximately 6.46 mmol/g at 50 bar, while CH₄ levels off at around 1.19 mmol/g. The uptake behaviour is characteristic of a Type I isotherm, governed by micropore filling and saturation effects. ZIF-8’s narrow pore aperture (3.4 Å) is highly size-selective, favouring CO₂ over CH₄ due to CO₂’s smaller kinetic diameter (3.3 Å) and higher quadrupole moment. Across the pressure range, CO₂ uptake in the binary mixture is consistently 20–40% lower than in the pure-component case. For example, at 10 bar, the CO₂ loading drops from 5.56 mmol/g (pure) to 3.15 mmol/g (IAST), a 43% suppression.This reflects the competitive nature of the binary system, where CH₄ molecules, though weakly adsorbing, still occupy valuable pore space and disrupt the otherwise higher capacity seen in unary systems.CH₄, on the other hand, experiences a much stronger reduction in uptake. At 10 bar, its loading falls from 2.09 mmol/g (pure) to 0.78 mmol/g (IAST) — a 63% drop. This is expected given its lower affinity and greater steric resistance within the ZIF-8 framework. Importantly, CH₄ adsorption is not only limited by physical size constraints but also outcompeted energetically by CO₂ molecules, which preferentially occupy the highest-energy adsorption sites.Despite these mutual suppressions, CO₂ remains the dominant species in the adsorbed phase across the entire pressure range. This is reflected in the adsorbed-phase mole fractions and the sustained selectivity values (discussed further in Section 3.3.3).In summary, IAST predictions reveal the nuanced effects of competitive adsorption, which are often missed in single-component evaluations. This introduces uncertainty due to possible variations in actual unit cell occupancy, framework rigidity, and measurement conditions.The dataset covers a narrow pressure range (1–8.2 bar), whereas the Tóth model used in IAST was fitted over a much broader 0.01–50 bar window, based on consensus pure-component data.Despite these caveats, these comparisons provide a valuable real-word checkpoint for evaluating model credibility under equimolar feed conditions. Fit Context: Tóth Model for Experimental ComparisonThe Tóth model used to generate the IAST predictions in this section was fitted to the same 1–8.2 bar pressure window as the experimental data. CO₂ is always dominant, but IAST underestimates both CO₂ and CH₄ adsorption relative to experiment, despite matching the trend. The divergence between the IAST model and the experimental reality is a classic example of applying a rigid model to a flexible material. Numerous studies have established that many metal-organic frameworks are "stimuli-responsive”, meaning their structure changes in response to external factors like guest adsorption45. The behaviour of ZIF-8 is the primary cause for the deviations seen./Figure 11 : IAST vs Experimental Binary Adsorption at yCO₂ = 0.5Figure 12 shows selectivity trends diverge subtly. While IAST predicts a steady increase in α_CO₂/CH₄, the experimental trend shows a peak at intermediate pressure, then decline. The reason IAST underestimates the amount of both gases adsorbed due to the well-documented “Swing effect" of ZIF-8's imidazolate linkers. This flexibility allows the framework’s windows to open, permitting the adsorption beyond what the rigid crystal structure suggests. IAST is built on pure-component isotherms, which are based on the initial state of the adsorbent./Figure 12 : IAST vs Experimental Binary Adsorption Selectivities at yCO2= 0.5However, the work by Fairen-Jimenez et al.  demonstrates that to accurately model the adsorption process in ZIF-8, two different structural configurations are necessary: one for the empty structure (ZIF-8AP) and another for the gas-loaded, high-pressure state (ZIF-8HP)46. Because IAST has no knowledge of this adsorption-induced structural transition to a different phase with a potentially larger capacity, it logically under-predicts the total uptake, just as seen in Figure 11. The mismatch in selectivity trends is a more nuanced outcome of the same phenomenon. IAST assumes a simple, static competition for a fixed set of adsorption sites. The experimental data, however, suggests a more dynamic process. The selectivity first rises as CO₂ preferentially adsorbs to the initial ZIF-8AP framework. The peak and subsequent decline likely mark the pressure where the transition to the ZIF-8HP configuration becomes significant. This structural change alters the adsorption landscape, creating new, energetically different adsorption sites, such as Site IV within the 4-ring window, as Fairen-Jimenez et al. identified46,47. As the energetic properties of these new sites are different from the original ones, the change in the competitive environment means the relative preference for CO₂ over CH₄ may decrease in the flexible, high-pressure structure. This causes the experimental selectivity to plateau or decline, a complex behaviour that cannot be predicted by the rigid, single-state assumptions inherent to IAST. Both discrepancies thus point to a single root cause: the failure of the ideal model to account for the real-world flexibility of the adsorbent framework.Conclusion and Future Work Conclusion This project successfully developed and applied a computational workflow to evaluate the potential of ZIF-8 for post-combustion carbon capture, specifically targeting CO₂/CH₄ separation. The methodology began with a rigorous statistical reproducibility analysis of pure-component isotherm data from the NIST database, leading to the construction of high-consistency consensus isotherms for both gases. Following a systematic comparison of isotherm models, the Tóth model was identified as the most appropriate, combining excellent numerical fit with physical interpretability. Using these validated fits, Ideal Adsorbed Solution Theory (IAST) was employed to predict binary adsorption. The model captured key thermodynamic trends, including preferential CO₂ uptake and pressure-enhanced selectivity, reinforcing ZIF-8’s theoretical promise for CO₂/CH₄ separation. However, benchmarking against the sole available experimental binary dataset revealed a key limitation: IAST systematically underestimated total gas loading and failed to reproduce the observed non-monotonic selectivity trend. This discrepancy is attributed to IAST’s rigid-adsorbent assumption, which fails to account for the guest-induced structural flexibility (“swing effect”) known to occur in ZIF-8. As a result, this project concludes that while IAST remains a valuable tool for preliminary material screening, its predictive accuracy is subjective for flexible frameworks like ZIF-8.Crucially, the rigorous methodology established in this study provides a robust and transferable template for evaluating other candidate carbon-capture materials, helping to systematically identify top performers from a foundation of reliable data.Future WorkBased on the findings and limitations identified in this study, the following avenues for future work are recommended to build a more complete and accurate understanding of ZIF-8's separation performance.Acquisition of Robust Experimental Binary DataThe comparison in this work was limited by the reliance on a single, unreproduced binary dataset covering a narrow pressure range limiting the generalisability of conclusions drawn about IAST’s performance for flexible MOFs. A crucial next step is to generate new, high-quality experimental binary adsorption data for the CO₂/CH₄/ZIF-8 system. These experiments should span a wider range of pressures (e.g., up to 50 bar) and feed compositions. Ideally, this data would be generated by multiple independent laboratories to enable reproducibility analysis performed here on the pure-component data, thereby creating a "gold standard" binary dataset for model validation.Advanced Molecular Modelling with Flexible FrameworksTo overcome the primary limitation of IAST, future computational work should employ more advanced simulation techniques that can explicitly account for adsorbent flexibility. In addition to GCMC, analytical models like the Osmotic Framework Adsorbed Solution Theory (OFAST) should be investigated.  OFAST is specifically designed for flexible materials and could provide a less computationally intensive method for predicting mixture behaviour in systems exhibiting guest-induced structural transitions. The use of Grand Canonical Monte Carlo (GCMC) simulations is recommended. This approach can model the framework's structural transitions directly, for instance, by allowing the simulation to sample both the initial (ZIF-8AP) and high-pressure (ZIF-8HP) configurations as described by Fairen-Jimenez et al46.  Such simulations would directly test the hypothesis that the "swing effect" is responsible for the observed deviations and could yield predictions that are in much closer agreement with experimental reality.Investigating the Impact of Process ImpuritiesTo assess industrial viability, ZIF-8's performance under realistic conditions must be tested. Future work should investigate the impact of common impurities, particularly water, on CO₂/CH₄ separation. Ternary simulations and experiments would reveal the material's tolerance to water, a critical factor for real-world applications where feed streams are rarely pure.Application of the Workflow to Novel Materials and SystemsThe methodology established in this project—combining data curation, statistical analysis, robust model selection, and predictive theory—has proven to be a powerful and effective workflow for evaluating adsorbent materials. A logical extension would be to apply this workflow which will be made open-sourced to other promising materials for carbon capture, such as other ZIFs, zeolites and MOFs (e.g., Mg-MOF-74) allowing for a direct comparison between ZIF-8's behaviour and the equilibrium-driven separation of materials with high-affinity open metal sites. Furthermore, the framework could be applied to other industrially relevant gas mixtures, such as CO₂/N₂ for post-combustion flue gas treatment, to broaden its impact in the search for next-generation separation materials.References(1)	Ritchie, H.; Rosado, P.; Roser, M.
